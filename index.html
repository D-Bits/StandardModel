The Standard Model

The Standard Model of Particle Physics, or simply The Standard Model, is a collection of the most important elementary particles and their fields. The describe the basic constituients of matter, energy, and the fundamental forces of nature. Because of it's success in describing such a wide array of physical phenomenon, it is sometimes called a "near theory of everything". However, many questions remain that are unanswered by the Standard Model, such as: dark matter, dark energy, quantum gravity, the mass of the Higgs, why there are three generations of matter particles/fields, and more.

Quarks are matter particles/fields that interact with the strong nuclear force. The 1st generation quarks (the up and down quarks) are the quarks that make up protons and neutrons. There are still four more quarks, spanning 2 more generations.

Learn more about the quarks

Leptons are matter particles/fields that do not interact with the strong nuclear force. The most common, and familiar, of the leptons is the electron. The electron also has two most massive sybllings, and their neutrally charged (neutrino) counterparts.

Learn more about leptons

Gauge Bosons are the particles/fields that communicate 3 of the 4 fundamental forces of nature at a quantum level: the electromagnetic force, the weak nuclear force, and the strong nuclear force. There is wide speculation about a force carrier for gravity called the graviton, but it has yet to be found in experiments, and does not fit well with the Standard Model.

Learn more about Gauge Bosons

The Higgs Boson/Higgs Field is the odd ball of the Standard Model. It is in a league of it's own.
